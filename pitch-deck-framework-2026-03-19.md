# Pitch Deck Framework — Pre-Seed B2B SaaS
**Version:** 2026-03-19
**Scope:** Pre-seed stage, B2B software companies

---

## Overview

The ideal pre-seed B2B software pitch deck runs **10-15 slides**, tells a narrative in three acts, and front-loads the strongest signal — whether that's traction, team, or a burning problem.

Investors spend roughly **2-4 minutes** reviewing an emailed deck before deciding whether to take a meeting. The purpose of the deck at pre-seed is not to close a round — it is to earn a second conversation.

---

## The 12-Slide Framework

### Slide 1: Title / Cover

**What it contains:**
- Company name and logo
- 5-7 word tagline that explains what you do
- Formula (500 Global): "A [product type] to help [target customer] with [problem] by [benefit]"
- Founder contact info

**Pre-seed note:** The tagline does the heavy lifting. If someone can't explain what you do after reading this slide, the deck has already failed (Techstars).

---

### Slide 2: Problem

**What it contains:**
- A specific, visceral customer pain point — not a macro industry trend
- One great statistic (a16z: "one great stat beats five forgettable ones")
- How the problem is currently solved (badly) today
- Frame from the customer's perspective

**B2B software note:** Tie the problem directly to a line item the buyer cares about — lost revenue, wasted time, compliance risk, or operational cost. Enterprise buyers purchase aspirin, not vitamins.

**Common mistake:** Techstars identifies broad industry analysis as the most common failure mode on this slide.

---

### Slide 3: Solution

**What it contains:**
- Value proposition in plain language
- Before/after comparison (a16z recommended format)
- One core idea — not a feature list
- Focus on what the user accomplishes, not what the product does

**Pre-seed note:** You may not have a finished product. Describe the solution conceptually but make it concrete. Link to a short demo video if one exists (Techstars recommends this).

---

### Slide 4: Why Now?

**What it contains:**
- The market timing argument for why this company should exist today
- Valid timing arguments (First Round Capital):
  - Problem was previously too expensive to solve
  - Enabling technology didn't exist until recently
  - Problem recently became more acute or pervasive
- Concrete market shifts: a new regulation, platform change, behavioral trend, or technology unlock

**Critical insight:** 75% of pitch decks omit or underdeliver this slide (Focused Chaos, 50-deck review). NFX identifies "timing" as one of only six things a VC needs to know. Skipping this slide makes your idea feel optional rather than urgent.

---

### Slide 5: Market Size

**What it contains:**
- TAM, SAM, and SOM
- Bottom-up sizing: number of target customers x annual contract value x purchase frequency
- Third-party research for top-down validation (Sequoia recommends both approaches)
- Markets should exceed $1B to interest most VCs (500 Global)

**B2B software note:** Be precise about your ideal customer profile. "All mid-market companies" is too broad. "US healthcare organizations with 200-2,000 employees that lack a dedicated compliance team" is credible.

**DocSend 2024 data:** VCs spend 19% less time on pre-seed market size slides — make this sharp and move on. Focus on SAM over TAM (Forum Ventures).

**Common mistake:** The "if we just get 1%" fallacy (Techstars) — citing a trillion-dollar market without a credible capture path.

---

### Slide 6: Product

**What it contains:**
- Real screenshots, wireframes, or a product walkthrough
- Simplified workflow diagram or bulleted steps if the UI is complex
- For B2B: illustrate user workflow and integration into existing systems

**DocSend data:** VCs spend approximately **77 seconds on product slides** — more than any other section.

**Pre-seed note:** Even pre-launch companies should show something tangible — a working prototype, a Figma mockup, or a clear product architecture.

**Common mistake (YC, Kevin Hale):** Screenshots that are illegible or overly complex.

---

### Slide 7: Business Model

**What it contains:**
- Pricing model: subscription, usage-based, or tiered
- Average contract value
- Theoretical unit economics: CAC, LTV, payback period assumptions
- One primary revenue stream (Reid Hoffman: multiple streams signal confusion)

**DocSend data:** VCs spent **48% more time on business model sections** in recent years.

**B2B software note:** Your business model must align with your go-to-market motion. You can't have an inside sales team for a $5/month product. For B2B SaaS, MRR or ARR is the north-star metric investors expect.

---

### Slide 8: Traction

**What it contains:**
- Paying customers, revenue, MoM growth rate
- LOIs, signed pilot agreements, design partners
- Waitlist size or conversion rates from a landing page test
- Named client logos (500 Global: put metrics in a chart)
- Avoid vanity metrics: downloads, page views, or social followers without context

**DocSend data:** Investors spend **110% more time** scrutinizing traction slides on unsuccessful decks vs. successful ones — confused or weak traction data actively hurts.

**B2B software note:** Gold standard at pre-seed B2B is signed LOIs or paid pilots with named customers. If pre-revenue, show structured customer discovery: how many interviews, what you learned, what conversion signals you've observed.

---

### Slide 9: Go-to-Market Strategy

**What it contains:**
- Your specific plan for reaching customers
- Your wedge: the specific entry point into the market
- Initial channel hypotheses and what you've tested
- Early data on CAC, conversion rates, expected payback (even if estimated)

**B2B software note:** Are you doing founder-led sales into SMBs? Partner-channel into enterprise? PLG with a self-serve tier? Show that your GTM strategy matches your price point and buyer persona.

**Common mistake:** Roughly 40% of pitch decks skip this slide or offer a buzzword list — "SEO, content marketing, partnerships" — with no specifics (Focused Chaos). Jerry Chen at Greylock: "Obsess about distribution as much as product."

---

### Slide 10: Competition

**What it contains:**
- Competitive landscape and your differentiation
- 2x2 quadrant or feature comparison table (with meaningful axes)
- Framing (500 Global): "Unlike [alternatives], [your product] [primary differentiator] and [secondary differentiator]"
- Your moat: proprietary data, integrations, technical IP, or network effects

**DocSend data:** VCs spend **48% less time** on seed-stage competition slides — investors care more about whether you acknowledge competition than about a detailed breakdown.

**Common mistake:** Never say you have no competitors. Every source flags this as the single most common red flag. Every problem has incumbent solutions, even if they're spreadsheets and email.

---

### Slide 11: Team

**What it contains:**
- Founders, key hires, and why this specific team is uniquely positioned to win
- Faces, names, roles, and a one-liner on relevant experience per person
- LinkedIn links (Forum Ventures)
- Unfair advantage framing (500 Global): experienced entrepreneurs, deep tech experience, industry insiders, or proven sales leaders

**DocSend 2024 data:** VCs spent **30-40% more time on team slides** at pre-seed and seed stages vs. prior year.

**Pre-seed note:** At pre-seed, the team essentially is the investment. If your team is your strongest asset, consider moving this to slide 2 or 3 (Ada Ventures, Crunchbase). Reid Hoffman: "Lead with your most compelling element."

---

### Slide 12: The Ask

**What it contains:**
- Dollar figure you're raising (not round nomenclature)
- Use of funds in 3-4 categories (e.g., engineering 50%, sales 30%, operations 20%)
- Milestones the funding will enable
- A discussion starter or question that invites brainstorming (First Round Capital)

**Pre-seed note:** Tie the ask to clear, achievable milestones — typically reaching a specific revenue target, closing a set number of customers, or achieving product-market fit signals that justify a seed round.

**Important:** Never pitch your valuation until you have a term sheet (Techstars).

---

## Slide Order Flexibility

The order above is a baseline, not a rigid rule. Lead with your strongest signal.

- If your team is exceptional: move team to slide 2
- If you have notable traction: surface it early
- If the problem is visceral and urgent: open with a customer story

The narrative arc matters more than the exact slide sequence.
