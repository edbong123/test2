# Common Pitch Deck Mistakes — Pre-Seed B2B
**Version:** 2026-03-19
**Sources:** DocSend data, Focused Chaos (50-deck review), Ada Ventures, Entrée Capital, Techstars, Upmetrics, Crunchbase

---

## Overview

Nine mistakes recur with striking consistency across reviewed decks. Drawn from DocSend fundraising data, VC blog posts, and a systematic review of 50 real pitch decks.

---

## The Nine Mistakes

### 1. No "Why Now?" Slide

**Frequency:** ~75% of decks omit or underdeliver this slide (Focused Chaos)

Without a timing argument grounded in a concrete market shift, the opportunity feels like it could wait — which means investors will wait too. This is the highest-leverage fix for most pre-seed decks.

---

### 2. Vague or Missing Go-to-Market Plan

**Frequency:** ~40% of decks phone this in

Buzzword lists like "SEO, content marketing, partnerships" with no specifics do not count. B2B investors specifically need to see that founders understand distribution, the buyer journey, and sales economics.

The fix: describe your exact wedge, your specific channel hypotheses, and what you've already tested.

---

### 3. Claiming No Competitors Exist

**Frequency:** Universally flagged as the single biggest red flag

Every problem has incumbent solutions, even if they are spreadsheets and email. Claiming no competition signals either naivety or lack of market research.

The fix: acknowledge alternatives and frame your differentiation clearly.

---

### 4. Walls of Text and Poor Design

**Rules (Kevin Hale, YC):** Legible, simple, obvious

Maximum 30 words per slide (Entrée Capital). Inconsistent fonts, pixelated logos, and cluttered layouts bias investors to assume your product is equally sloppy. Over a third of reviewed decks contained typos (Focused Chaos).

---

### 5. Vanity TAM Numbers Without Grounding

Citing a trillion-dollar market with no credible path to capture even a fraction — what Techstars calls the "if we just get 1%" fallacy. Bottom-up SAM sizing is far more credible than top-down TAM claims.

---

### 6. Burying the Team Slide

At pre-seed, the team is frequently the entire investment thesis. Putting it at the end — after 10 slides of product and market — means your strongest signal comes last.

If your team is exceptional, move it to slide 2 or 3.

---

### 7. Unrealistic Financial Projections

At pre-seed with no revenue, detailed five-year projections are actively harmful. They signal that founders either don't understand their business or are trying to impress with numbers that can't be validated.

Replace with: unit economics assumptions, pricing logic, and milestone-based use of funds.

---

### 8. Generic Deck Sent to Every Investor

Investors can tell when they're receiving a bulk distribution. Personalization — referencing a portfolio company, a relevant thesis, or a specific partner's background — materially improves response rates.

---

### 9. Missing Traction at Pre-Seed

Zero evidence of customer contact is a hard stop for most VCs, even at pre-seed. The bar is not high — named interviews, a signed LOI, or a paying beta user all qualify.

If you have nothing, run five customer discovery conversations before sending the deck.
