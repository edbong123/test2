# Narrative and Storytelling in Pitch Decks
**Version:** 2026-03-19

---

## Overview

The best pitch decks are not checklists — they are stories. Multiple frameworks across VC blogs, pitch coaches, and successful founders converge on a three-part narrative structure.

Messages delivered as narratives are **22x more memorable** than facts alone.

---

## The Three-Act Structure

### Act 1: Make them care (Hearts)
- Open with something visceral and urgent
- Establish the status quo and conflict
- Hook the investor emotionally before making any analytical claims

### Act 2: Make them understand (Minds)
- Introduce the solution and product
- Present market evidence and business model
- Build the rational case for why this works

### Act 3: Give them reason to bet (Wallets)
- Deliver the team and traction
- Make the ask
- End with a question or statement that opens dialogue

This maps directly to Ben Yoskovitz's "Hearts → Minds → Wallets" framework, widely cited in the VC community.

---

## Slide Headline Advice

Do not use obvious section headers like "Problem," "Solution," "Team." It kills the narrative flow (BenchSci CEO, raised $40M+).

Instead, use **descriptive headlines that advance the story**. Each slide's header should state the key takeaway, not the category.

- Instead of "Problem" → "Finance teams waste 12 hours a week reconciling what one tool should handle"
- Instead of "Solution" → "One dashboard that replaces 9 tools and closes reconciliation in minutes"
- Instead of "Market Size" → "$4.2B addressable market, growing 18% YoY"

## Cognitive Science Behind Pitching

Oren Klaff (*Pitch Anything*) argues that pitches are first processed by the brain's primitive "reptilian" region, which scans for threats and opportunities. Simple, urgent, emotionally resonant messaging gets through; dense analytical slides do not. The analytical case comes later, once attention is captured.

Key takeaway: **your first 30 seconds determine whether you'll be heard for the rest of the pitch.**

---

## Two Versions of Every Deck

Techstars makes a critical distinction between two required formats:

| Version | Purpose | Design principle |
|---|---|---|
| "Read me" deck | Emailed to investors | Stands alone without your voice. More detail per slide. |
| "Listen to me" deck | Used in live presentation | Fewer words. Supports what you say, doesn't replace it. |

The email deck must sell without your voice. The presentation deck should not be read — it should be a backdrop.

---

## Delivery Principles

- **Practice extensively.** First Round's Oren Jacob (former Pixar CTO): "You have to know it so well that it seems spontaneous."
- **Steer into objections.** Reid Hoffman: address the 1-3 objections investors are most likely to have, and do it early when you have maximum attention.
- **Your objective is the next meeting**, not closing a deal. Techstars' Nicole Glaros: "Capture attention in the first 30 seconds."
- **5-7 core ideas** is the limit (YC's Kevin Hale): "There are probably 100 reasons your company is great, but people can only remember a few."

---

## Format Recommendations

- Maximum **30 words per slide** (Entrée Capital)
- Legible, simple, obvious — three design rules from Kevin Hale (YC)
- Budget **$100-300 for a freelance designer** (Forum Ventures) — signals seriousness
- Deck length: **10-15 slides** core narrative, optional appendix for detail-hungry investors
